The Villager

At the edge of the Chetambe Hills, you feel like you are at the edge of the world. Looking down, you see the River Nzoia meandering through the hills to a steep vertical river bed, now known as the Nabuyole Falls. To the east, you can see the Nandi Escarpment in the neighboring county, a county known for its endless fields of tea. When the paper factory was here, a pine forest took over the westward slopes. Of course, I know this is not the etdge of Earth, but this place at the edge of my village still takes my breath away. This is Webuye in Bungoma County. When I was last standing there, I had just been admitted to KenSAP (Kenya Scholar Access Program). I sat on the cliffs, observing daily life below me and reminiscing about how far I had come. It was the onset of the rainy season and the scent of guava trees filled the air with the fragrance of the ripe fruits. Monkeys were chattering boisterously while jumping from tree to tree. Brown mushrooms sprouted from the spongy, moist grounds. As a child, we found out the hard way which ones made for a nice delicacy or a day of stomach pains. As I walk along the hills, I see an old cargo train spewing smoke and slowly crawling toward the Uganda border. In the distance, people without money chased the train along the tracks for the chance to jump onto the sides for a free ride. On the plains below, I noticed the deserted paper factory, now only used for criminal activity and drug abuse. This factory had once been the source of livelihood for the vast majority of Webuye's residents. Before the factory, this village was a forest and I fear that its closing down will mean the fall of Webuye. In the bush, behind me on the cliffs, some whispers bring me back from my memories. “Ni uria watira masomo vilayi.” (He is the one who got everything in school). I turn around and see children staring at me and I give them some words of wisdom about doing well in school. 3 Nowadays, with the factory closed, most families struggle to make ends meet by selling crops or quarrying stones from the Chetambe Hills. My father used to work casual jobs like this to make ends meet until his demise. I remember the day he died like it was yesterday. Earlier that month, my father was falsely diagnosed with tuberculosis. We later found out he died of a preventable illness. I was six years old and saw no hope in the future. We were riddled with debt from his medical bills.

Primary School

When he died, I was forced to switch schools to a local public primary school, where education was free. We had to move into one of the abandoned paper factory rooms, surrounded by poverty and lost hope. But I maintained hope. My mother always told me, “education is the only way out of poverty.” But education was not easy here. We walked three kilometers to a dilapidated school that did not have enough books, desks, or teachers to support us. After school, we fetched water and firewood, weeded the garden, swung our jembes (garden hoe) to dig holes, and found work at the quarry. Yet I always remembered that education was our only chance. Life was difficult but school was simple. I found myself in harmony with my academics. I developed a special interest for Mathematics that still sticks with me today. I fell in love with learning how numbers ruled the world around me and I started to explore the world around me in a scientific manner. The hills became opportunities to calculate the rate of flowing water, the garden became a place for imagining the laws behind biological form, and our expenses became a way for me to plan family finances. When my primary school results were released, it was a frenzied celebration for the whole village.

Secondary School

I was selected to join Alliance High School, hundreds of kilometers away from Webuye. The villagers were happy; one of their own had a chance to bring back the fortunes of the distant land. They pooled together enough money to support my first year at Alliance High School and a few weeks later, my mother took me to high school. The journey from home to Alliance cut through seventeen counties. We arrived at Alliance High School almost a day after departing Webuye. I was thrilled to be attending Kenya’s best high school, a school that would open up a whole new world of possibilities for a better future. Almost immediately, I found myself facing a whole new set of difficulties adjusting to Alliance but as I walked through those gates, I felt accomplished - like I had found a new edge of the world. I left my mother at the gate, but not before she reminded me that education was our hope for a better future

Kenya Scholar Access Program

After graduating from Allince High School, I applied and got in to the Kenya SCholar Access Program, KenSAP, where I applied to college and got in.

Moringa School

When KenSAP asked me if I was willing to join the famous Moringa school, I quickly accepted the offer. I was extremely passionate about enrolling since this would help me be at par with other freshmen in my college who have backgrounds in IT. Since I intend to persue something in the line of Computer Technology, Moringa School and its rigorous curriculum was the best place to start. At Moringa school, I intend to harness the opportunity to learn how to learn and influence the next generation of coders. I also am ready to work hard to make coding a source of income in my future years. Moreover, staying at home for the next 6 months would be boring, and the social interaction and collaboration at Moringa school would do my social life great.
